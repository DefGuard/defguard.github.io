As an IT administrator, you've heard the complaints every day. "The VPN is slow," "My files are taking forever to download," "I got disconnected... again."

You've spent hours, maybe days, digging through logs, tweaking MTU settings, and checking firewall policies, convinced it's a configuration error.

We’re here to tell you: **It's not your fault.**

You are trying to optimize a foundation that was built for a different internet. The problem isn't your settings; it's the 20-year-old protocol you're forced to use.

## Why You Can't Make Your FortiGate VPN Faster

This is the "networking rabbit hole." When a user complains about a `FortiClient slow connection`, we've all followed the checklist:

* Is it the user's home internet?
* Is the firewall appliance overloaded?
* Is it a routing issue?

You're digging for a networking problem, but the real culprit is a *protocol* problem. Most legacy enterprise VPNs, like the common [SSL VPNs](https://openvpn.net/) on many all-in-one boxes, are built on protocols (like TCP) that are two decades old. They were designed for a world where "remote access" meant checking email, not running video calls and transferring multi-gigabyte files.

You are stuck debugging a symptom. The disease is the technical debt of that legacy protocol.

The available data shows three groups of remote access systems.

1.  **Legacy Enterprise (SSL VPN / IPsec):**
    Most current products — from Fortinet to OpenVPN — use older tunneling protocols that are being phased out for technical and security reasons.

2.  **Peer-to-Peer Mesh (WireGuard®):**
    Tools such as Tailscale and NetBird use WireGuard® but are designed for peer-to-peer connectivity, not as central gateways.

3.  **Integrated Access Platforms:**
    Systems such as [Defguard](https://defguard.net/) use the same modern protocol to provide centralized access control with built-in identity and MFA.

The boundaries between these groups are now visible in real deployments.
* Legacy systems are reaching performance and compliance limits.
* **Fortinet has announced the phase-out of SSL VPN tunnel mode** starting with FortiOS 7.6.3, citing security and stability issues.
* The Norwegian National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) gives similar guidance, recommending a move from SSL VPN to stronger protocols such as IPsec IKEv2.

These changes indicate a shift in how secure access is designed — from perimeter-based VPNs toward models that verify every connection instead of assuming trust.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Why is my Fortinet VPN so slow?
Your Fortinet VPN is likely slow due to the underlying SSL VPN protocol. It wasn't designed for modern high-bandwidth traffic and suffers from performance issues like "TCP-over-TCP," which creates significant latency. The problem isn't your settings; it's the protocol's technical debt.

### How can I make my FortiGate VPN faster?
While you can tweak MTU settings or firewall policies, these are minor fixes for a foundational problem. The most effective way to get a faster VPN is to switch to a modern protocol. Even Fortinet is deprecating SSL VPN tunnel mode, signaling a move away from the protocol.

### What is the 'TCP-over-TCP' problem in SSL VPNs?
The "TCP-over-TCP" problem occurs when you run TCP-based traffic (like a file transfer) over a TCP-based VPN (like SSL VPN). When a packet is lost, *both* layers try to retransmit, creating a cascade of latency and poor performance. This is a primary cause of 'slow' and 'unstable' connections.

### Is Fortinet deprecating SSL VPN?
Yes, Fortinet has announced the phase-out (deprecation) of SSL VPN tunnel mode starting in FortiOS 7.6.3. This move cites security and stability issues and strongly indicates that the industry, including legacy vendors, is moving away from SSL VPN toward more modern and secure protocols.

### What is a faster alternative to Fortinet SSL VPN?
The leading faster alternative is a solution built on the WireGuard® protocol. WireGuard® is a modern VPN protocol that is UDP-based, eliminating the "TCP-over-TCP" problem. Enterprise-grade solutions like Defguard use WireGuard® to provide a faster, more stable, and more secure remote access gateway.
