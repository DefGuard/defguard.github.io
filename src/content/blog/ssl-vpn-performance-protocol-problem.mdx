As a system administrator, you already know how this story goes. A user opens a ticket: "The VPN is slow." Another one: "My files take forever to download." And then the classic: "It disconnected again."

You check the logs. You adjust the MTU. You review firewall rules and routing. 

Everything appears correct but yet the symptoms keep coming back.

When these issues appear during packet loss, jitter, short connectivity gaps, or when the client switches networks, the cause often lies below policy and routing. 

To understand why these problems reoccur, we need to examine the SSL VPN transport layer, the part of the stack where most of these effects originate.

## The Root Cause: How TCP-over-TCP Kills Performance

Many SSL VPN deployments, including FortiClient and OpenVPN when running in TCP mode, rely on TLS-over-TCP as their transport. This design has known limitations when the network becomes unstable or changes during use.

Two properties of this model directly shape how SSL VPN behaves under those conditions:

**Nested transport semantics**

Encapsulating TCP inside TCP causes both layers to run congestion control independently. Outer TCP hides packet loss, inner TCP reacts to delay as congestion, and the two feedback loops can trigger severe throughput collapse under even small loss.

**Dependency on Static Transport Addresses**

A TLS session depends on a stable combination of source IP, source port, destination IP, destination port and protocol. Any change in these parameters breaks the transport and invalidates the session.

At this point, many administrators naturally point to DTLS as the alternative transport. Several remote-access platforms (including Cisco AnyConnect, FortiClient/FortiGate) support Datagram TLS (DTLS) alongside TLS-over-TCP.

DTLS is the UDP-based variant of TLS: it preserves the same cryptographic properties but does not rely on a reliable, ordered TCP stream.

However, the practical impact of DTLS in SSL VPN deployments reduces to two observable behaviours:

**Fallback when UDP is unavailable**

If a network blocks or filters UDP, the client attempts DTLS, receives no usable response and switches to TLS-over-TCP. The VPN stays connected but now runs in TCP-over-TCP mode, which degrades throughput under packet loss or jitter.

**No mobility**

A DTLS session is still tied to a static network identity. When the client changes networks or interfaces, this tuple changes and the session must be re-established, which users experience as a disconnect and reconnect cycle.

## Why DTLS Is Only a Partial Solution

Taken together, these behaviours show that DTLS removes only the TCP-in-TCP interaction. It does not change the fact that both TLS-over-TCP and DTLS depend on a transport session that must remain tied to the same network parameters for its entire lifetime. 

When those parameters change, for example, because the client switches networks or its interface resets then the session cannot continue and must be renegotiated.

With DTLS fallback and this session model explained, we can now focus on the transport path that administrators encounter most often in practice: TLS-over-TCP, either because DTLS was never enabled or because the client has silently fallen back to it. 

The recurring issues seen in production stem from this transport behaviour rather than from firewall rules or policy settings.

## Observable Issues in SSL VPN: What Administrators Actually See

User complaints about slow or unstable SSL VPN connections can be directly related to four specific, measurable transport behaviours that might be observed in logs and troubleshooting workflows in SSL VPN deployments:

**Throughput collapse under packet loss:**

Even minor packet loss (on the outer TCP path) can cause significant throughput degradation for TCP-over-TCP tunnels, because both TCP layers independently perform retransmissions and congestion control in response to the same loss events. Fortinet describes packet-loss-related SSL VPN performance symptoms in its [official troubleshooting guidance](https://community.fortinet.com/t5/FortiGate/Troubleshooting-Tip-How-to-troubleshoot-SSL-VPN-link-packet-loss/ta-p/411275).

**Roaming failures when the client changes networks:**

If the client moves between networks such as Wi-Fi, LTE and wired LAN, the outer TLS session breaks because it is bound to a specific five-tuple (source IP, source port, destination IP, destination port, protocol) A change in source IP or interface invalidates that connection unless additional mobility mechanisms are present.

**Session resets on short connectivity interruptions:**

Short outages, sleep/wake events, or network interface resets invalidate the TCP connection, requiring full renegotiation. The SSL VPN tunnel then has to be re-established and re-authenticated, which users experience as a disconnect and reconnect cycle. ([Sophos documents similar disconnect/reconnect behaviour](https://docs.sophos.com/nsg/sophos-firewall/20.0/Help/en-us/webhelp/onlinehelp/AdministratorHelp/RemoteAccessVPN/TroubleshootRASSLVPN/RASSLVPNTunnelsDisconnect/index.html) in its SSL VPN troubleshooting guide, including cases where tunnels reset during short outages or endpoint sleep/wake).

**TCP-over-TCP debugging complexity:**

When a TCP stream is encapsulated inside another TCP connection, retransmissions, timeouts, and congestion responses can originate from either layer. This makes it harder to attribute observed latency or throughput problems to a specific point in the stack. Fortinet explains the sensitivity of SSL VPN tunnels to latency and retransmission behaviour [in this technical note](https://community.fortinet.com/t5/FortiGate/Technical-Tip-Why-SSL-VPN-tunnels-are-more-sensitive-to-latency/ta-p/214663).

These transport behaviours aren't specific to a single vendor or configuration. They also appear in publications from national cybersecurity agencies and in vendor documentation, which point to similar limitations of SSL-based remote-access designs in unstable or high-latency networks.

## Why Agencies and Vendors Recommend Alternatives to SSL VPN

This protocol-level picture is consistent with what several national authorities and at least one major vendor have published in recent years:

[Fortinet has announced](https://docs.fortinet.com/document/fortigate/7.6.4/fortios-release-notes/173430/ssl-vpn-tunnel-mode-replaced-with-ipsec-vpn) the phase-out of SSL VPN tunnel mode in specific FortiOS releases (for example, 7.6.3) and instructs customers to migrate these tunnel-mode configurations to IPsec VPN before upgrading.

Global security agencies, including [NSA and CISA](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/alerts/2024/06/18/cisa-and-partners-release-guidance-modern-approaches-network-access-security) in the United States, [advise organisations to minimise the public exposure of SSLVPN/WebVPN interfaces](https://media.defense.gov/2021/Sep/28/2002863184/-1/-1/0/csi_selecting-hardening-remote-access-vpns-20210928.pdf) and to use well-established VPN protocols such as IKEv2/IPsec, together with strict endpoint hardening

[The Norwegian National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) has published guidance](https://thecyberexpress.com/replacement-of-sslvpn-and-webvpn/) recommending a full phase-out of SSLVPN/WebVPN and migration to protocols with clearer isolation boundaries, such as IPsec IKEv2, citing repeated exploitation of SSLVPN/WebVPN edge services.

These recommendations show a consistent direction: organisations are encouraged to minimise exposure of SSLVPN/WebVPN services and to rely on transport protocols with clearer isolation boundaries and more predictable behaviour under loss and network changes.

This documented guidance does not predict the long-term future of SSL VPN, but it provides a clear reference point for evaluating alternatives that address the transport constraints highlighted earlier.

## The WireGuard® Advantage: A Stateless, UDP-First Architecture

WireGuard® provides a useful reference point for this comparison because it is a modern protocol built from first principles, designed to operate over UDP with minimal state. 

It avoids nested TCP interactions and does not bind session validity to a fixed client IP/port, which changes how remote-access tunnels behave under real-world mobility and instability.

Taken together, these transport properties explain why WireGuard® often behaves more consistently than the TLS-over-TCP transport model when the network introduces loss, roaming events or short interruptions.

These behaviours follow directly from the transport design: UDP without connection state, identity tied to cryptographic keys rather than socket parameters, and a fixed cryptographic suite without negotiation.

## Why WireGuard® is Not a Full VPN Enterprise Solution

However, transport is only one part of a remote-access VPN. A transport protocol defines how packets move, but it does not define how users authenticate, how policies are evaluated or how access is audited. 

These functions belong to the remote-access gateway — the central point where VPN sessions terminate and where identity and policy checks take place.

### Migrating from SSL VPN to WireGuard®

Using a modern UDP-based protocol removes the transport-layer limitations of SSL VPN (such as performance bottlenecks and dropped sessions during roaming), but it does provide the identity, MFA and policy functions required in a remote-access setup.

Replacing SSL VPN therefore requires two separate choices:

1. selecting the protocol for the data plane (for example, IKEv2 or WireGuard®), and
2. selecting a product that implements that protocol within your organisation's identity, MFA, policy and audit model.

WireGuard® provides only the transport layer. Solutions built on top of it differ in how they handle remote access: some create peer-to-peer overlays, some operate as a single-node server, and others offer a central termination point where identity, MFA and policy checks take place.

To understand which tools can fulfil this role in practice, we can compare how these approaches behave under real conditions.

For organisations that operate remote access through SSL-based VPNs from vendors such as Fortinet (FortiGate VPN), Cisco (AnyConnect) or SonicWall, moving to a different transport protocol is not only a matter of changing the tunnel technology.

These deployments often depend on a set of operational capabilities that sit above the transport layer, and any migration must preserve this functional completeness:

- user, group and policy management (RBAC),
- multi-factor authentication,
- integration with enterprise identity sources (such as Active Directory or LDAP),
- centralised auditability and access logging,
- Hub-and-spoke style of VPN architecture

These capabilities allow organisations to adopt WireGuard® for remote access without losing identity integration, MFA, policy enforcement or centralised access control. At the same time, Defguard reduces operational overhead by providing a unified management interface and simplified provisioning workflows.

For administrators, the practical benefit is a remote-access setup that requires less manual intervention, produces fewer reconnect-related issues and is easier to maintain at scale, while end users experience a faster and more stable connection due to the properties of the WireGuard® transport.

## Summary

In practice, many performance and stability issues in SSL-based remote-access VPNs originate from the transport model rather than from firewall rules or user configuration.

When the outer TCP session encounters packet loss, latency variation, or an IP/port change during roaming, the TLS-over-TCP tunnel reacts with retransmissions, timeouts or a full session reset because both TLS and TCP maintain state that depends on a stable, loss-free connection.

Even DTLS mode reduces only part of this behaviour, since the session still depends on a fixed 5-tuple and must be re-established when the client's network changes.

Replacing the transport removes these constraints, but a transport protocol on its own does not provide the operational capabilities required for remote-access VPNs.

WireGuard® solves the transport-layer problems, but it does not provide identity, MFA, policy evaluation, auditing, or controlled entry points, all of which are required in an enterprise environment.

Defguard combines WireGuard®'s transport performance with the functions required to manage enterprise remote access consistently: user and group management, built-in or external IdP integration, MFA at connection time, policy enforcement and auditable access.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### 1. Why is the SSL-VPN on my FortiGate slow?

FortiGate supports DTLS (UDP-based) for improved performance, but many networks, such as public Wi-Fi, guest VLANs, corporate outbound filters or mobile hotspots — block UDP. When that happens, FortiClient silently falls back to TLS-over-TCP.

In fallback mode, TCP application traffic is encapsulated inside another TCP stream. Both layers react independently to packet loss and congestion, which can significantly reduce throughput even when the firewall and MTU are correctly configured.

This behaviour is not specific to Fortinet; it is inherent to any SSL VPN operating in TLS-over-TCP mode.

Platforms built on UDP-based transports (such as WireGuard®) do not rely on this fallback mechanism and are less affected by outbound-UDP filtering, which helps maintain performance and stability across varied networks.

### 2. How can I make my FortiGate SSL-VPN faster?

MTU changes or firewall adjustments can improve edge cases, but they do not remove the limitations of TCP-over-TCP encapsulation. Fortinet's own documentation shows that SSL VPN throughput and stability issues persist even with correct configuration. The only structural improvement is to switch to a protocol that avoids nested TCP, such as IKEv2/IPsec or WireGuard®.

### 3. Is SSL-VPN being deprecated on FortiGate?

Yes. Fortinet has begun phasing out SSL VPN tunnel mode in FortiOS 7.6.x and instructs administrators to migrate these tunnels to IPsec. This change is specific to tunnel mode and is justified by Fortinet on the basis of stability and security behaviour.

### 4. What is a faster alternative to FortiGate SSL-VPN?

The most efficient alternative is a VPN built on a UDP-based protocol, such as WireGuard®. WireGuard® removes TCP-over-TCP interactions, maintains session continuity across IP changes, and operates with minimal state. Enterprise platforms such as Defguard use WireGuard® as the transport layer and add identity, MFA, policy enforcement, and auditing required in a remote-access gateway.

### 5. Does WireGuard® support MFA or SSO for enterprise remote-access VPNs?

WireGuard® as a protocol does not implement MFA, SSO, user authentication, or session authorisation. It authenticates peers solely by static key pairs and does not contain a mechanism to verify user identity at connection time. Therefore, MFA and SSO must be implemented by a platform that wraps identity checks around the WireGuard® tunnel. Defguard provides this layer by enforcing MFA and identity binding before issuing WireGuard® configuration or allowing a session.

### 6. Can WireGuard® replace FortiGate SSL-VPN for enterprise remote access?

WireGuard® can replace the transport layer, but not the gateway layer. The protocol provides encrypted UDP tunnels with good mobility properties, but it does not include gateway functions such as user identity, access policies, logging, or centralised auditing. To function as a remote-access replacement for SSL VPN, WireGuard® must be combined with a gateway-style platform (such as Defguard) that handles identity, MFA, routing policies, and audit controls.
